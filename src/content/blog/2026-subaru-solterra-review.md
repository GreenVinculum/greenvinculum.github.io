The 2026 Subaru Solterra is not trying to be a spaceship on wheels. It is an electric SUV that mostly behaves like the kind of car Subaru people already know — just quieter, smoother, and plugged in. After putting real miles on it, here is an honest snapshot of what works and what still needs work.

## Range that holds up in the real world

On a full charge, I see about **317 miles** with climate control off. Turn the HVAC on — which, for most of us, is most of the time — and that settles around **285 miles**. That is a meaningful drop, but the climate-on number is the one I actually plan trips around, and it has been reliable enough that range anxiety has not been a daily problem.

During summer driving in regular traffic at roughly **65 mph**, with climate on, I average about **4.5 mi/kWh**. That efficiency makes the usable range feel honest rather than optimistic. You are not babying the throttle or drafting semis to get there; it reflects normal commuting and highway miles.

## Charging: fast when you need it

At a Tesla fast charger, going from **20% to 90%** takes me about **19 minutes**. For road-trip stops, that is short enough to grab coffee and get back on the road without turning every charge into a production. Your network and station condition will vary, but the curve on this car is practical for the way most people actually travel.

## What Subaru got right

**Buttons.** Real ones. Climate, volume, shortcuts — you can operate a lot of the car without hunting through nested touch menus. In a segment obsessed with glass slabs, that alone makes the Solterra feel less fatiguing on long drives.

**It drives like an SUV, not a gadget.** The Solterra does not ask you to relearn what a car is. It has the upright seating, familiar controls, and calm road manners you would expect from a compact crossover — only the drivetrain is electric. If you want an EV that does not constantly remind you it is an EV, this lands in a sweet spot.

## Where it falls short

**No proper Dog Mode or Camp Mode.** Other EVs bake these into the UI: leave the climate on, lock the screen, walk away. Here you can still make it work, but you have to **work around** the software — hold power, fiddle with settings, and hope you remembered the sequence. For a car pitched at outdoor people and dog owners, that is a miss.

**No towing capacity — for now.** If you need a hitch for a small camper, utility trailer, or bike rack that carries real weight, you are out of luck at the moment. Fine for many commuters; a dealbreaker for others.

## Bottom line

The 2026 Solterra is a sensible electric daily driver with strong real-world range, efficient summer highway numbers, and fast-charging that fits road trips. It feels familiar in the best Subaru way — capable, honest, not flashy — and the physical controls are a genuine quality-of-life win.

The gaps are mostly software and capability: pet and camping modes should be one tap, not a workaround, and towing would unlock a huge slice of the Subaru audience. As it stands, this is an easy recommendation for someone who wants an EV that behaves like a normal SUV first and a tech demo second.

**Verdict:** A solid, lived-in EV with excellent range and charging — held back by missing convenience features and no tow rating.
